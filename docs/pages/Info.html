The Road Not Taken

Robert L. Frost

Robert Lee Frost was an American poet who lived during the 19th and 20th centuries. His literary contributions were recognized with numerous accolades. Frost won four Pulitzer Prizes for his remarkable works, including "New Hampshire" in 1924, "Collected Poems" in 1931, "A Further Range" in 1937, and "A Witness Tree" in 1943. His exceptional talent was also acknowledged with prestigious awards such as the Congressional Gold Medal and the Edward MacDowell medal in 1962, and the Bollingen Prize in 1963. Additionally, Frost was nominated for the Nobel Prize in Literature an impressive 31 times. Furthermore, he received over 40 honorary degrees from esteemed institutions, including Harvard, Princeton, Oxford, Cambridge, and more. Notably, Frost holds the distinction of being the only person to receive two honorary degrees from Dartmouth.

Early Life and Education

Robert Lee Frost was born on March 26, 1874, in San Francisco, California. His formative years were spent in San Francisco until 1886 when his family moved to Lawrence, Massachusetts, following the death of his father due to tuberculosis. Frost's affinity for poetry emerged during his time at Lawrence High School, where he published his first poem in the school's magazine. Graduating in 1892, Frost briefly attended Dartmouth College before returning home to teach and explore various jobs. However, he soon realized that his true calling lay in writing poetry. In 1894, Frost published his first poem titled "My Butterfly. An Elegy" and a year later, he married Elinor M. White. Although Frost attended Harvard University in 1897, his studies were interrupted by illness, leading to his departure after two years.

Finding Inspiration and Teaching

Following his time at Harvard, Frost spent nine years working on a farm in Derry, New Hampshire, a property his grandfather had purchased for him and his wife. It was during this period that Frost penned many of his renowned poems. However, the farm's lack of success led Frost to teach English at several schools in the New Hampshire area. In 1912, he embarked on a significant change by moving with his family to England. It was in England where Frost published two poetry collections, "A Boy's Will" in 1913 and "North of Boston" in 1914. With the outbreak of the First World War in 1915, Frost returned to America and purchased a farm in Franconia, New Hampshire, known today as "The Frost Place," a museum and nonprofit educational center for poetry. In 1916, Frost received the prestigious honor of becoming an honorary member of "Phi Beta Kappa" at Harvard, solidifying his scholarly recognition. From 1917 to 1920, Frost taught English at Amherst College in Massachusetts, where he encouraged his students to capture the essence of spoken English in their writing, an approach he termed "the sound of sense." He further pursued his teaching career with a fellowship teaching post at the University of Michigan from 1921 to 1927, after which he returned to teach at Amherst. Frost also devoted his summers and autumns from 1921 to 1962 to teaching at the "Bread Loaf School of English" at Middlebury College.

Personal Loss and Enduring Legacy

Tragedy struck Robert Frost's life in 1938 when his wife, Elinor, passed away due to heart failure. Despite this profound loss, Frost continued to make significant contributions to the literary world. In 1961, he had the honor of performing a reading at the inauguration of President John F. Kennedy, a testament to his enduring influence and popularity. Sadly, Robert Frost himself passed away on January 29, 1963, in Boston, Massachusetts, due to complications from prostate surgery. His departure marked the end of an era for American poetry. Today, Frost's legacy lives on through his profound impact on the literary landscape. Robert Frost is buried in the Old Bennington Cemetery in Vermont, where his grave bears the inscription, "I had a lover's quarrel with the world," the poignant final line from his poem "The Lesson for Today." Despite his untimely departure, Frost's literary achievements continue to captivate readers and inspire generations to come. In addition to his illustrious career, Frost was also a family man. He and Elinor had six children, although tragically, only two outlived him. Their family bonds and the influence of Frost's poetic genius created a rich and enduring legacy that is cherished to this day.

The poem

During his time in England (1912-1915) Frost became a close friend of Edward Thomas, a British poet. They took many walks together, and one day they came across two roads. Thomas was indecisive about which road to take, and would later lament that they should have taken the other one. This event inspired Frost to write “The Road Not Taken”. After returning to America he sent Thomas an advance copy of the poem. Some believe that the poem might have been significant in Thomas’ decision to enlist in the first world war. He was killed two years later in the Battle of Arras.
